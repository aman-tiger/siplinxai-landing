**TL;DR:** Effective meeting notes capture decisions, action items, and key context—not a transcript of everything said. The hardest part is doing this while staying present. AI note-takers solve the split by handling documentation automatically, so you can focus on the conversation.

## The Core Problem with Meeting Note-Taking

Taking notes during a meeting creates a fundamental split: you're either listening and contributing, or you're writing. You can't fully do both.

Most people resolve this by writing less—and end up with notes that are too thin to be useful a week later. Others over-correct and try to capture everything, missing the thread of the conversation while they type.

AI meeting note tools eliminate this tradeoff entirely. But even with AI assistance, understanding what good meeting notes look like helps you review, edit, and use AI-generated output effectively.

---

## The Right Framework: What to Capture

Meeting notes should answer five questions:

1. **What was decided?** Explicitly capture decisions. "We're going with vendor A" is a decision. "We discussed vendor options" is not.

2. **Who's doing what?** Action items need names. "The team will follow up" is not an action item. "Sarah will email vendor A by Wednesday" is.

3. **What's the deadline?** Every action item needs a timeframe—even a rough one.

4. **What was the context for key decisions?** A future reader needs enough context to understand why a decision was made, not just what it was.

5. **What's unresolved?** Open questions that need follow-up deserve their own section.

---

## Common Note-Taking Mistakes to Avoid

**Capturing discussion instead of decisions.** Meeting notes aren't a screenplay. "John said X, then Maria disagreed, then they discussed Y for 15 minutes" is rarely useful. What was decided at the end of that discussion?

**Writing during the meeting without a clear structure.** Notes taken linearly from a conversation are hard to use. Use a template with sections (decisions, action items, context) rather than a stream of consciousness.

**Waiting until after the meeting to write.** Memory degrades fast. Notes written an hour after a meeting are already missing context. Real-time capture—whether by hand or AI—is always better.

**Not sharing notes promptly.** Notes that sit in someone's personal document and aren't shared with attendees within a few hours are only marginally better than no notes at all.

## How AI Note-Taking Works

AI meeting tools handle the documentation layer automatically:

1. **Real-time transcription** — Speech is converted to text as the meeting happens, with speaker labels.
2. **Decision extraction** — An LLM identifies explicit decisions from the transcript.
3. **Action item extraction** — Commitments with owners and timelines are extracted and structured.
4. **Summary generation** — A concise narrative of what was discussed is produced.
5. **Export** — Notes are available in text, Markdown, or shareable format immediately after the meeting.

The result is the equivalent of having a skilled note-taker in every meeting—one who never gets distracted, never misattributes a quote, and delivers the notes the moment the call ends.

---

## When Human Note-Taking Still Matters

AI handles documentation well, but some contexts benefit from a human layer:

- **Nuance and subtext:** AI captures what was said. A skilled human note-taker can also capture what was implied, what body language suggested, or what wasn't said.
- **Priority filtering:** AI may surface ten action items when three are the ones that actually matter. A human reviewer adds judgment about priority.
- **Sensitive interpretation:** In negotiations or conflict resolution, what something *means* politically or interpersonally may be as important as what was said.

The best practice: use AI for the documentation layer, then spend five minutes reviewing and annotating the output with your own context before sharing.

## Recommended Note-Taking Structure

```
MEETING: [Title]
DATE: [Date] | ATTENDEES: [Names]

DECISIONS
- [Decision 1]
- [Decision 2]

ACTION ITEMS
- [Task] | Owner: [Name] | Due: [Date]

KEY CONTEXT
- [Important background for future reference]

OPEN QUESTIONS
- [Unresolved items for follow-up]
```

This structure works whether you're writing manually or reviewing AI-generated output.
