## Why Action Items Fall Through the Cracks

A meeting ends. Everyone agrees something will happen. Three days later, nothing has happened.

This is not an accountability problem. It's a documentation problem. In the absence of a clear, shared record of who committed to what, action items exist only in the individual memories of attendees—and memory degrades fast.

Research on forgetting curves suggests people retain about 50% of new information after an hour and roughly 25% after a day. By the time the next meeting rolls around, the specifics of what was agreed are often genuinely unclear—not from negligence, but from how human memory works.

AI action item extraction solves this at the source: it captures commitments as they're made, during the meeting, before memory has a chance to degrade.

---

## What Makes a Good Action Item

The best action items follow a simple formula:

**[Action verb] + [deliverable] + [owner] + [deadline]**

Examples:
- ✅ "Draft the Q3 proposal — Maria — by Friday"
- ✅ "Schedule follow-up call with procurement — James — this week"
- ❌ "Someone look into the pricing thing" (no owner, no deadline, vague deliverable)

Good AI tools identify action items using this pattern—looking for commitment language ("I'll", "we should", "let's make sure", "by end of week") and extracting the surrounding context to fill in owner and timeline.

---

## How AI Extracts Action Items from Meetings

The process has three steps:

**1. Transcription.** The meeting is converted to text in real time using speech recognition. Speaker labels are attached so "I'll handle that" can be attributed to the right person.

**2. Semantic analysis.** A large language model reads the transcript and identifies segments containing commitments, tasks, and follow-up obligations. It distinguishes between discussion ("maybe we should consider...") and commitments ("I'll send that over today").

**3. Structuring.** The LLM formats each identified commitment as a structured action item: task description, assigned owner (from speaker label), and deadline (extracted or inferred from context).

The best tools produce action items that don't require cleanup—they read like the list a meticulous project manager would produce after sitting through your meeting.

## Best Practices for AI-Extracted Action Items

**Be explicit in meetings.** AI extracts what's in the transcript. If a commitment is vague ("let's figure out the timeline"), the extracted action item will be vague too. Cultivating the habit of explicit commitment language ("I'll confirm the timeline and send an update by Thursday") dramatically improves AI output.

**Assign ownership out loud.** "James will handle the vendor outreach" gives the AI an owner to extract. "Someone should handle vendor outreach" doesn't.

**Review before sending.** AI extraction is not perfect. Review the action item list before sharing it with the team, and correct any misattributions or vague descriptions.

**Export to your task system.** Action items on a page nobody revisits aren't useful. The best workflow: extract from AI, export to your project management tool (Notion, Linear, Jira, Asana), and assign due dates in context.
